    "Probabiler, Codétenu, Trois Chambrées"

    Breaking it down:

    - Probabiler — not a real French word, but instantly parsed: "to probabilize." A verb that doesn't exist in any language, implying the act of making things probabilistic. It sounds like it should exist. That's the hook.
    - Codétenu — real word: "fellow inmate" / "co-detainee." But here it plays double: co- (together) + détenu (held/contained). Three libraries held together. Three comrades in the same cell, working the same problem. There's a hint of
    defiance — these aren't polite collaborators, they're inmates who broke out of Python's GIL.
    - Trois Chambrées — real military term: soldiers sharing barracks. You've been using this. It works because it's warm (comradeship) but structured (military quarters). Three libraries, one mission.

    Probabilerer — not real French, but delightfully broken French in the spirit of how occupations are formed.
    French occupation nouns typically follow patterns like:
    - -eur / -euse: programmeur, danseuse
    - -ier / -ière: boulangier, pompier
    - -iste: probabiliste (that's the real one — a probability theorist)

    So "probabilerer" sounds like someone took probabilité and jammed a Germanic -er suffix onto it — the way English does with "programmer" or German does with "Programmierer." It's a beautifully mangled Franco-Germanic hybrid: "one who 
    probabilizes."
